EMERGENCY: SNAKE BITE
This is a medical emergency. Follow these steps immediately:

Immediate Actions

  • Keep the person calm and still to slow the spread of venom.
  • Remove any tight clothing or jewelry near the bite area.
  • Keep the bitten area below heart level if possible.
  • DO NOT cut the wound, suck out venom, apply ice, or use a tourniquet.
  • DO NOT give the person alcohol, caffeine, or pain medication.
  • Transport the person to a medical facility immediately.
  • If possible, take a photo of the snake (from a safe distance) to help with identification and treatment.
  • Monitor breathing and be prepared to perform CPR if necessary.
Important: Snake bites can be life-threatening. Antivenom is often required and must be administered by medical professionals. Every minute counts - seek medical help immediately!
DOG BITE - RISK OF INFECTION AND RABIES

Immediate Actions

  • Wash the wound immediately with soap and running water for at least 5 minutes.
  • Apply gentle pressure with a clean cloth if bleeding.
  • Apply antibiotic ointment and cover with a sterile bandage.
  • Elevate the injured area if possible.
  • Seek medical attention immediately for proper wound care and rabies assessment.
  • If possible, get information about the dog's vaccination status.
  • Rabies post-exposure prophylaxis may be required if the dog's status is unknown.
  • Tetanus booster may be needed if not up to date.
Important: Dog bites can lead to serious infections including rabies, which is almost always fatal if not treated promptly. Medical evaluation is essential within 24 hours.
SEIZURE/EPILEPSY EPISODE

What to Do During a Seizure

  • Keep the person safe by clearing the area around them of hard or sharp objects.
  • Place something soft under their head and turn them gently onto one side.
  • Do NOT restrain the person or put anything in their mouth.
  • Time the seizure - if it lasts longer than 5 minutes, seek emergency help.
  • Stay with the person until the seizure ends and they are fully awake.
  • After the seizure, let the person rest in a safe position.
  • Seek medical evaluation to determine the cause and appropriate treatment.
  • If this is the person's first seizure, medical evaluation is essential.
Important: Seizures can have many causes including epilepsy, infections, or other medical conditions. Proper diagnosis and treatment are essential to prevent future episodes.
FAINTING (SYNCOPE)

Immediate Actions

  • If someone has fainted, lay them down and elevate their legs above heart level.
  • Loosen any tight clothing, especially around the neck.
  • When they regain consciousness, keep them lying down for a few minutes.
  • Help them sit up slowly, then stand slowly to prevent another episode.
  • Offer water or a sugary drink if available.
  • Seek medical attention to determine the cause, especially if it's the first episode.
  • Note any warning signs before fainting (dizziness, nausea, sweating) to report to a doctor.
  • Avoid triggers like standing for long periods, dehydration, or sudden position changes.
Important: While often benign, fainting can sometimes indicate a serious underlying condition like heart problems. Medical evaluation is recommended, especially for recurrent episodes.
EMERGENCY: POSSIBLE STROKE
Remember FAST: Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty, Time to call emergency services.

Immediate Actions

  • Call emergency services immediately - every minute counts!
  • Note the time when symptoms first appeared - this is critical for treatment.
  • Keep the person still and comfortable while waiting for help.
  • DO NOT give them anything to eat or drink.
  • If they are unconscious but breathing, place them in the recovery position.
  • If they stop breathing, begin CPR if you are trained.
  • Do not give any medication, including aspirin, until evaluated by medical professionals.
  • Stay with the person and monitor their condition until help arrives.
Important: Stroke is a medical emergency. Rapid treatment can minimize brain damage and potential complications. Do not wait to see if symptoms improve - seek help immediately!
BURN TREATMENT

Immediate Actions

  • Cool the burn immediately with cool (not cold) running water for at least 10 minutes.
  • Remove any clothing or jewelry near the burn area (unless stuck to the skin).
  • Cover the burn with a sterile non-stick dressing or clean cloth.
  • DO NOT apply ice, butter, oil, or ointments to severe burns.
  • DO NOT break blisters if they form.
  • Take paracetamol for pain relief if needed.
  • Seek medical attention for large burns, burns on face/hands/feet/genitals, or deep burns.
  • Keep the person warm to prevent hypothermia (especially children and elderly).
Important: For chemical burns, brush off dry chemicals first, then rinse with water for at least 20 minutes. For electrical burns, ensure power source is off before touching the person.
WOUND CARE

Proper Wound Care

  • Wash your hands before treating the wound.
  • Stop bleeding by applying gentle pressure with a clean cloth or bandage.
  • Clean the wound with clean water - avoid using hydrogen peroxide or iodine.
  • Apply antibiotic ointment if available.
  • Cover with a sterile bandage or dressing.
  • Change dressing daily or when wet/dirty.
  • Watch for signs of infection: redness, swelling, increased pain, pus, or fever.
  • Seek medical attention for deep wounds, animal bites, puncture wounds, or signs of infection.
Important: Tetanus vaccination is recommended if you haven't had a booster in the last 5-10 years, especially for dirty or deep wounds.
